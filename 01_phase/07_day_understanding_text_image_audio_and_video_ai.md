# Phase 1 -> Day 7  
## Understanding Text, Image, Audio, and Video AI  
### Trainer Session Notes

**Session Duration:** 1 hour 30 minutes  
**Session Mode:** 20% Theory | 80% Hands-On  
**Primary Tools for Today:** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, Grok  
**Secondary Demo Types:** Image generation or analysis tools, speech/transcription tools, video AI examples where available

---

## 1. Session Goal for the Trainer

By the end of this session, participants should:
- understand that modern Artificial Intelligence is no longer limited only to text
- understand the practical meaning of text AI, image AI, audio AI, and video AI
- recognize the kinds of tasks each modality can handle well
- understand that multimodal Artificial Intelligence systems can work across more than one content type
- develop realistic judgment about capability, usefulness, and risk across different media forms
- understand that output quality, review needs, and trust risks vary by modality

**Your teaching objective:**  
Today is about expanding the learner’s mental model beyond chat.  
Do not make this session tool-demo entertainment only.  
Teach it so the learner feels:  
“Now I understand that Artificial Intelligence can work with text, images, audio, and video, and I can think clearly about what each modality is good at and where caution is needed.”

## 3. Opening Script Notes for Yourself

### What you should establish in the first few minutes
- participants may still think Artificial Intelligence mainly means chatbots
- today expands the understanding from “language model” to “media modality”
- modern Artificial Intelligence can process or generate more than text
- the key idea is that different modalities have different strengths, limits, and risks

### Suggested opening flow
You may say:

“Until now, most of our examples have focused on text, because text-based Artificial Intelligence is where most people begin. But modern Artificial Intelligence is not limited to text. It can also work with images, audio, and video.”

Then say:

“Today’s goal is not to memorize brand names. The goal is to understand capability by media type. What can Artificial Intelligence do with text? What can it do with images? What can it do with speech or audio? What can it do with video? And what changes when one system works across multiple forms?”

Then add:

“If we do not build this understanding, we end up treating every tool as ‘just another chatbot.’ That is an incomplete view.”

## 4. Core Theory You Must Cover Briefly

Keep this section practical and structured.

### 4.1 Text AI
Use this explanation:

**Text AI works with written language. It can generate, summarize, classify, rewrite, extract, compare, explain, translate, and organize textual content.**

Typical tasks:
- drafting
- summarization
- note cleanup
- question answering
- translation
- extraction
- classification
- outline creation
- briefing support

### 4.2 Image AI
Use this explanation:

**Image AI works with visual content such as photographs, diagrams, screenshots, scanned material, or generated images. It may analyze images, describe them, classify what is present, detect objects, or generate images from prompts.**

Typical tasks:
- image description
- visual analysis
- object detection
- document image understanding
- prompt-to-image generation
- image enhancement
- layout interpretation

### 4.3 Audio AI
Use this explanation:

**Audio AI works with sound and speech. It can transcribe speech into text, summarize spoken content, detect speaker patterns in some systems, generate speech, and support voice-driven interaction.**

Typical tasks:
- transcription
- meeting notes from speech
- voice-to-text
- audio summarization
- text-to-speech
- spoken command interaction

### 4.4 Video AI
Use this explanation:

**Video AI works with moving visual content, and in some cases with the combination of visual frames, sound, and language. It may summarize video content, identify events, generate video, or analyze patterns in video streams.**

Typical tasks:
- video summarization
- event extraction
- scene understanding
- caption generation
- synthetic video generation
- multimodal media review

### 4.5 Multimodal AI
This is the key idea for today.

Use this explanation:

**Multimodal Artificial Intelligence means a system can handle more than one type of input or output, such as text plus image, or image plus audio, or text plus vision.**

Then simplify:

**A multimodal system can understand or generate across multiple content forms.**

Examples:
- upload an image and ask text questions about it
- provide audio and ask for summary
- analyze a screenshot and explain what it shows
- generate an image from a text prompt
- summarize a video clip into text notes

### 4.6 Important balance point
Do not imply that all tools do all modalities equally well.

Clarify:
- some tools are strongest in text
- some are strong in image generation
- some are strong in transcription
- some handle multiple forms but still vary by task quality

### 4.7 Why this matters professionally
Explain:
- real work is not only text
- field work often includes photos, scans, audio notes, and videos
- multimodal AI can reduce friction across different media forms
- but trust and verification still matter across all modalities

Use these repeatedly:

**Artificial Intelligence is no longer text-only.**

**Different modalities have different strengths.**

**Text AI works with language, image AI works with visuals, audio AI works with sound, and video AI works with moving visual content.**

**Multimodal systems combine more than one content type.**

## 6. Concept Explanation You Must Deliver Clearly

### 6.1 The simplest mental model
You should say:

“Think of modality as the form in which information exists. If the information is written, that is text. If it is visual, that is image. If it is spoken, that is audio. If it is moving visual content, that is video. Modern Artificial Intelligence can increasingly work across all of these forms.”

### 6.2 Another simple explanation
You may say:

“A text model helps you work with words. An image model helps you work with visuals. An audio model helps you work with speech and sound. A video-capable system helps you work with moving visual material. A multimodal system connects these worlds.”

### 6.3 Important caution
Also say:

“More modalities do not automatically mean more reliability. In fact, as capability expands, the importance of review, verification, and judgment also increases.”

### 6.4 Important modern insight
Say this clearly:

“One of the biggest shifts in Artificial Intelligence is that systems are moving from single-modality tools toward multimodal capability. That means work is becoming less separated by media type.”

Use this table.

|---|---|---|---|
| Text AI | written language | drafting, summarizing, rewriting | hallucination, omission |
| Image AI | photos, diagrams, screenshots | analysis, description, generation | misidentification, visual ambiguity |
| Audio AI | speech and sound | transcription, summarization, voice interaction | transcription errors, speaker confusion |
| Video AI | moving visual content | summarization, event review, generation | missed context, synthetic manipulation |
| Multimodal AI | combined content types | richer tasks across media | more complexity, more review needs |

Then add below:

**As modality expands, usefulness expands, but so do interpretation and trust challenges.**

## 8. Demo 1 — Text AI and Image AI Comparison

### Purpose
To show that different modalities solve different kinds of tasks.

### What to do
Take one text task and one image-related task.

### Text task example
“Summarize these rough notes into a professional brief.”

### Image task example
Upload a screenshot, form, photo, chart, or diagram and ask:
“Describe what is visible here and extract the key points.”

If image generation is available, you may also demonstrate:
“Generate a simple conceptual image showing data flow from field input to decision dashboard.”

### What participants should observe
- text AI is strongest when the problem is language-based
- image AI is useful when visual interpretation is needed
- the format of input changes the type of capability needed
- image understanding is not the same as text summarization
- prompt clarity still matters across modalities

### Verbatim you can say
“When the input itself changes form, the model behavior needed also changes. A text-focused task asks the system to process language. An image-focused task asks the system to interpret or generate visual information. This is why modality matters.”

### Key insight to emphasize
**Different input forms require different AI strengths.**

---

## 9. Demo 2 — Audio AI and Transcription / Summarization

### Purpose
To show how Artificial Intelligence handles spoken content differently from written content.

### What to do
Use a short audio clip, spoken note, or example transcript.

Tasks to demonstrate:
- transcribe speech into text
- summarize spoken content into key points
- convert audio into action items

### Example prompt after transcription
“Summarize this spoken content into three key decisions and two next steps.”

### What participants should observe
- audio can be turned into usable text
- spoken content can become notes quickly
- transcription quality may vary
- accents, background noise, and unclear speech can reduce quality
- audio AI is highly useful but not perfect

### Verbatim you can say
“This is a strong practical use case. Speech is often unstructured and time-consuming to process manually. Audio AI can turn speech into text and then text into summary, which saves substantial effort. But errors in transcription can change meaning, so review still matters.”

### Key insight to emphasize
**Audio AI is highly useful for converting speech into usable written form.**

---

## 10. Demo 3 — Video AI, Multimodal Behavior, and Capability Comparison

### Purpose
To introduce video understanding and the idea of systems that combine multiple modalities.

### What to do
Use a video example, a video summary example, or a conceptual example if live video AI is not available.

Possible tasks:
- summarize what happens in a short video
- generate captions
- identify events or scene changes
- discuss video-generation capability conceptually
- compare how a multimodal system may use text + image + audio together

### What participants should observe
- video combines visual sequence and sometimes audio
- video understanding is more complex than single-image analysis
- multimodal reasoning can connect image, text, and audio information
- video and multimodal outputs need careful interpretation

### Verbatim you can say
“Video is not just image repeated many times. It includes sequence, time, movement, and often audio. That makes it a richer but also more complex modality for Artificial Intelligence.”

Then add:

“When a system can combine text, image, and audio understanding, it becomes multimodal. That increases usefulness, but it also increases the need for careful review.”

### Key insight to emphasize
**Multimodal systems are powerful because they connect information across media types.**

### 11.3 Audio AI strengths

### 11.4 Video AI strengths

## 12. Risks and Cautions by Modality You Must Mention

### 12.1 Text AI risks
- hallucinated facts
- false confidence
- incomplete reasoning
- generic phrasing

### 12.2 Image AI risks
- visual misinterpretation
- poor reading of low-quality images
- confusion in ambiguous visuals
- incorrect assumptions from a single frame

### 12.3 Audio AI risks
- transcription errors
- accent-related mistakes
- wrong segmentation of meaning
- missed nuance from low audio quality

### 12.4 Video AI risks
- loss of context
- missed events
- incorrect scene interpretation
- synthetic manipulation and deepfake concerns

### 12.5 Multimodal risks
- more content forms means more possible error points
- users may over-trust systems that appear more “human-like”
- complexity can create hidden mistakes

Use this line:

**More capability does not remove the need for review.**

## 13. Activity — Map Tasks to the Correct Modality

### Duration
11 minutes

### Objective
Help participants think in terms of media-task fit rather than only tool branding.

### Instructions
Ask participants to classify tasks into:
- text AI
- image AI
- audio AI
- video AI
- multimodal AI

### Example task list
1. Turn rough meeting notes into a briefing note.
2. Read a screenshot and explain what it shows.
3. Transcribe a spoken note and create action items.
4. Summarize a short video clip into key events.
5. Generate an image from a concept description.
6. Compare two photos and identify visible differences.
7. Convert a recorded explanation into a concise written summary.
8. Upload an image and ask questions about specific objects shown.
9. Produce a short caption for a video.
10. Explain a chart shown in an image.

### Suggested support prompt
“Map the following tasks to text AI, image AI, audio AI, video AI, or multimodal AI. Explain why.”

Use:
- ChatGPT
- Claude
- Gemini
- optionally compare with DeepSeek and Grok where relevant

### Verbatim you can say
“The goal is not to memorize which brand does what. The goal is to think correctly about the nature of the task. What type of content is involved? What modality is required? And does the task need one modality or more than one?”

### Trainer note
If someone says “this is AI,” ask:
“Yes, but what kind of AI capability is actually needed here?”

That deepens understanding.

## 14. High-Value Concepts to Emphasize During Discussion

### 14.1 Text is only one entry point
Explain:
- many learners start with chat
- but real-world workflows include photos, scans, voice, and video
- understanding modality expands practical use

### 14.2 Different media forms create different strengths and errors
Explain:
- text errors look different from image errors
- transcription errors differ from hallucinated summaries
- video interpretation carries its own ambiguity

### 14.3 Multimodal systems are strategically important
Explain:
- work rarely happens in one clean medium
- mixed-media workflows are common
- multimodal AI better matches real-world information flow

### 14.4 Visual and audio outputs can feel persuasive
Explain:
- a generated image may look authentic
- a generated voice may sound convincing
- a synthetic video may appear real
- this increases misuse risk

### 14.5 Capability and caution must grow together
Repeat:
- powerful tools require mature use
- media richness increases value but also increases trust risk

## 15. Exact Verbatim Blocks for the Trainer

Use these where useful.

### Verbatim Block 1 — Modality concept
“A modality is simply the form in which information exists. Text is one modality. Image is another. Audio is another. Video is another. Modern Artificial Intelligence can increasingly work across all of these.”

### Verbatim Block 2 — Text vs image vs audio vs video
“A text system helps us work with words. An image-capable system helps us work with visuals. An audio-capable system helps us work with speech and sound. A video-capable system helps us work with moving visual material, often together with audio.”

### Verbatim Block 3 — Multimodal AI
“When one system can understand or generate across more than one content form, such as text plus image or audio plus text, we call that multimodal Artificial Intelligence.”

### Verbatim Block 4 — Why this matters
“This matters because real work is not text-only. We deal with screenshots, scanned documents, spoken notes, photos, charts, and videos. Artificial Intelligence becomes more practical when it can handle the actual form in which information appears.”

### Verbatim Block 5 — Review discipline
“As capability expands from text into image, audio, and video, usefulness increases, but so does the need for careful review. Rich media can be persuasive even when it is imperfect or synthetic.”

## 16. Questions You Should Ask Throughout the Session

Use these to maintain engagement:
- What kind of content is this task based on?
- Which modality is actually required here?
- Is one modality enough, or is this a multimodal problem?
- What can go wrong in this modality?
- Would this output be safe to trust directly?
- How does review differ for text, image, audio, and video?
- What makes multimodal systems strategically more useful?

Use this line:

**As modality becomes richer, interpretation risk also becomes richer.**

1. Artificial Intelligence is no longer limited to text

- what value Artificial Intelligence might add
